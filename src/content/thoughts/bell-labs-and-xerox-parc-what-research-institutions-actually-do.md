The standard "Xerox fumbled commercialisation" framing misses the point entirely. The ideas diffused anyway — enabled Apple, Microsoft, and the entire PC revolution. Bell Labs: transistor, laser, Unix, C, information theory, cellular networks. Even after AT&T's breakup, the spillover sustained innovation for decades. The model influenced every modern research lab.

The pattern across all successful research institutions: Visionary or movement challenges status quo → creates alternative structure and infrastructure → gains traction, proves viability → gets partially co-opted or corrupted → but the ideas and tools persist and compound. You don't need perfect institutions. You need institutions that move the needle forward and leave infrastructure for the next generation.

What this means for building research institutions: The question isn't "will this be captured or commercialised?" — it will be, eventually. The question is "will the ideas and infrastructure survive and compound regardless?" Bell Labs was captured by AT&T's commercial interests. Unix escaped anyway. PARC's ideas were stolen by Apple. The GUI survived anyway. Stallman and FSF were opposed by every major corporation. Linux now runs most of the world's servers.

The three failure modes to avoid from PARC specifically: Building things too far ahead of the hardware (Smalltalk required machines that didn't exist affordably), lack of internal path from research to product, and parent company's interests actively misaligned with the research's implications. The Xerox failure was not that PARC didn't produce great ideas — it's that the organisation was structured so those ideas couldn't flow to impact.

The India-specific implication: Building a multi-specialisation applied research organisation with the Bell Labs / PARC model but organised around capability infrastructure for underserved populations rather than corporate profit, blue-sky prestige, or academic metrics. The organising principle changes which research gets prioritised, which problems get selected, and how success is measured.
