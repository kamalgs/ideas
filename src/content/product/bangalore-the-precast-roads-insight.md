---
title: "Bangalore Infrastructure — The Precast Roads Insight"
date: 2026-01-01
tags: [bangalore, infrastructure, precast, bbmp, urban-governance, india, roads]
---

White-topping roads in Bangalore takes 30-45 days per km. Precast concrete panels — factory-cured, trucked in at night, laid like tiles, grouted — achieve 3-5 days per km. The technology exists. L&T and Fuji Silvertech already do precast for metros and expressways in Bangalore. The application to municipal roads is missing.

The cost framing that kills it: BBMP sees 30-50% higher direct material cost for precast and stops there. What doesn't appear on their ledger: economic cost of traffic disruption (20,000+ vehicles/day for weeks = ₹5-10 crore in externalized costs per stretch), longer replacement cycles (precast lasts 1.5-2x longer, lifecycle cost comparable), fewer re-dos (cast-in-situ quality in Bangalore is terrible — rain during curing, wrong mix — "newly done" roads crumbling in 2-3 years).

Why Bangalore won't do it: No precast capacity nearby, BBMP tender documents mandate cast-in-situ so contractors bid accordingly, contractor ecosystem lacks precast capability and won't lobby for it, political will absent because the suffering is diffuse (commuters) and the benefit of change is invisible (who gets credit for less disruption?).

The governance failure insight: The daily commute pain is not an engineering constraint — it's a governance failure dressed as one. The contractor economics actively reward slow execution — paid on milestone completion, not time. Liquidated damages not enforced. Cast-in-situ is the rational choice for the contractor, the irrational choice for the city.

The broader frame: India's infrastructure plumbing — precast standardization, logistics, BBMP specification reform — is not addressable by "deeptech" and not demo-able to VCs. The real bottleneck is boring standards harmonization, not AI/ML. The venture capital going into deeptech is not touching this. The 100 km/day highway ambition would be more achievable with precast/prefab than with faster concrete pouring.
