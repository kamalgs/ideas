Lynch vs Bogle — The Real Passive vs Active Debate

investing 1 December 2025
investingpassive-investingactive-investingetfpmsboglelynchindia

The standard Bogle vs Lynch debate applied to a specific situation: someone who rejected PMS for a 7-ETF passive portfolio. Lynch’s sharpest challenge: you didn’t take control, you just changed which committee decides what you own — index committee instead of fund manager. You’re still delegating.

The uncomfortable Lynch question: If you’re Chief Architect at India’s largest brokerage, you have material informational edge on fintech infrastructure, payment flows, regulatory changes, which tech vendors are winning. Yet you’re buying 500 random companies by market cap including the 247th largest smallcap you can’t explain to a 10-year-old.

Bogle’s rebuttal holds: tyranny of compounding costs. At 3.5% all-in PMS fees vs 0.24% ETF, the PMS needs to outperform by 3-4% annually just to break even — and most don’t over full cycles. The fee drag on ₹2.5 Cr over 10 years: ₹18L ETF vs ₹1.2 Cr+ PMS.

The resolution that’s actually honest: These aren’t equivalent strategies on a spectrum — they’re different epistemic commitments. Passive indexing is a statement that you don’t have edge. Stock picking is a claim that you do. The uncomfortable middle (rejecting PMS but also not building a conviction portfolio) is neither.
